### major techniques of dimensionality reduction
- feature selection
    - e.g. in bag of words, we discard some of the words and keep only the most frequent ones. this is a form of feature selection.
- feature extraction
    - patterns, get rid of collinearity (but has its own risks, e.g. hard to interpret)

#### feature extraction vs selection
extraction:
- all orig features are used and are transferred
- the transformed features are linear/nonlinear combinations of the orinignal features
selection:
- only a subset of the original features are contained

## PCA
dimension reduction technique -- projects data onto a lower dimensional space while retaining as much variance as possible
- maximize the variance in the projected space
- has some assumptions that can affect its performance

### toy example
[img :
small mass attached to a spring, oscillating in 2d.
]
we want to find the direction of the oscillation, which is the direction of maximum variance. this is the first principal component. the second principal component is orthogonal to the first and captures the remaining variance.

we analyze it from 3 different angles, and take the temperature too. the temp doesnt do anything, but the 3 angles are correlated and we can find the direction of maximum variance in the 3d space. this is the first principal component. the second and third principal components are orthogonal to the first and capture the remaining variance. also very noisy -- angle differences, etc.

goal -- transform 7 dimensions down to 1 without loss of important information. this toy ex is analogous to every other problem

### benefits
pca helps to
- understand the data
- the pcs define how much of the original variables are expressed by them
- makes it clear which variables are correlated

### assumptions
3 strong assumptions:
+ linearity -- if the relationshiop between the vars are not linear, there are different solutions to apply. some of them derive from pca.
+ assumes large variances = importance --
+ assumes differences are important -- not always important

another: PC's are orthogonal -- not always the case in real data, and since pca is lossy, could do more harm than good. there are some techniques that relax this assumption, e.g. independent component analysis (ICA) TODO: verify
